# E02: Exposure Bias, Organic Conversion, and the Failure of Naive Lead Scoring

This experiment transitions the project from **predictive lead scoring** into **causal decision systems**.

E01 established that conversion models can successfully rank likely converters better than random allocation. However, high conversion probability alone does not imply that sales intervention caused the conversion.

This creates the central production problem explored in E02:

> Are sales teams prioritizing leads that truly require intervention, or are they mostly selecting leads that would have converted organically anyway?

This experiment studies the long-term limitations of production conversion-ranking systems and motivates the need for uplift modeling and policy learning in E03.

---

# 1. Motivation

Most real-world lead scoring systems optimize:

\[
P(Y=1 \mid X)
\]

where:

- \(X\) = lead features
- \(Y\) = observed conversion outcome

The operational policy is usually:

\[
S_B^{score}
=
\operatorname{TopB}_i(\hat{s}_i)
\]

meaning:

> Rank leads by predicted conversion probability and allocate limited sales capacity to the highest-scoring leads.

This appears reasonable because sales teams want more conversions.

However, this objective contains a hidden assumption:

> The leads most likely to convert are also the leads most influenced by intervention.

In practice, this assumption is often false.

---

# 2. The Core Causal Problem

A lead can convert for two fundamentally different reasons:

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Organic conversion | The lead would convert even without sales contact |
| Incremental conversion | The lead converts because intervention changed behavior |

Standard conversion models cannot distinguish these cases.

They only observe:

\[
Y_i
\]

not the counterfactual outcomes:

\[
Y_i(1), Y_i(0)
\]

where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(Y_i(1)\) | outcome if contacted |
| \(Y_i(0)\) | outcome if not contacted |

The incremental effect of intervention is:

\[
\tau_i
=
Y_i(1)-Y_i(0)
\]

or in expectation:

\[
\tau(x)
=
\mathbb{E}[Y(1)-Y(0)\mid X=x]
\]

This quantity is uplift, treatment effect, or incremental value.

---

# 3. Why Conversion Ranking Can Fail

Suppose two leads have the following properties:

| Lead | Organic Conversion | Sales Impact | Final Conversion Probability |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| A | 0.90 | +0.01 | 0.91 |
| B | 0.20 | +0.40 | 0.60 |

A conversion model ranks Lead A higher because:

\[
0.91 > 0.60
\]

But from a sales allocation perspective:

| Lead | Incremental Gain |
|---|---:|
| A | +0.01 |
| B | +0.40 |

The intervention is far more valuable for Lead B.

This creates a structural inefficiency:

> Conversion ranking systems tend to over-allocate resources toward already-converting users.

In marketing literature, these are often called:

- sure things
- organic buyers
- self-converters

Meanwhile, highly persuadable leads may receive lower scores because their baseline conversion probability is moderate.

---

# 4. Exposure Bias in Production Systems

Production sales systems introduce another complication:

\[
P(A=1 \mid X) \ne P(A=1)
\]

where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(A\) | intervention assignment |
| \(X\) | lead features |

Sales teams do not contact leads randomly.

Instead, they preferentially contact:
- high-intent leads
- high-value leads
- leads already likely to convert

This creates exposure bias or treatment-selection bias.

As a result:

\[
A_i \not\perp Y_i(0),Y_i(1)
\]

The treated population systematically differs from the untreated population.

This creates a feedback loop:

```text
Historical sales policy
    ->
Observed conversions
    ->
Future conversion model
    ->
More selection of already favored leads
```

Over time, the system reinforces existing allocation behavior.

The model learns:

> “These leads convert frequently.”

But it cannot determine whether:
- sales caused conversion
- or the leads were already highly likely to convert.

---

# 5. Production Interpretation

E02 reframes the lead scoring problem from:

> “Who converts?”

to:

> “Who converts because we intervened?”

This is the transition from prediction to causal allocation.

The distinction is critical because sales capacity is limited:

\[
\sum_i A_i \le B
\]

When intervention resources are scarce, maximizing raw conversion probability is not necessarily optimal.

The true optimization target is incremental impact:

\[
\max_\pi
\sum_i A_i \tau_i
\]

rather than:

\[
\max_\pi
\sum_i A_i P(Y_i=1\mid X_i)
\]

This distinction becomes the foundation of E03.

---

# 6. Goals of E02

E02 is designed to empirically demonstrate three hypotheses.

---

## H1: Conversion Probability and Incremental Impact Are Not Equivalent

A lead with high:

\[
P(Y=1\mid X)
\]

does not necessarily have high:

\[
\tau(x)
\]

Expected result:

- high-conversion leads often contain many organic converters
- uplift and conversion rankings diverge substantially

---

## H2: Historical Treatment Policies Create Selection Bias

Historical intervention assignment affects observed outcomes.

Expected result:

- treated leads appear more valuable even when treatment effect is weak
- naive estimators overestimate intervention effectiveness

---

## H3: Conversion Ranking Produces Lower Incremental Value Than Uplift-Based Allocation

Expected result:

\[
V(\pi_{uplift})
>
V(\pi_{conversion})
\]

under identical budget constraints.

---

# 7. Experimental Structure

E02 focuses on controlled causal benchmarking.

Unlike E01, which evaluates ranking quality, E02 evaluates intervention effectiveness.

---

# 8. Dataset Design

This experiment is best performed using:

1. Hillstrom randomized email dataset
2. Semi-synthetic uplift dataset

The semi-synthetic setup is especially important because true uplift is known.

---

## Semi-Synthetic Structure

Each lead contains:

| Variable | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(X_i\) | lead features |
| \(A_i\) | treatment assignment |
| \(Y_i(0)\) | organic outcome |
| \(Y_i(1)\) | treated outcome |
| \(\tau_i\) | true uplift |
| \(Y_i\) | observed factual outcome |

Observed outcomes follow:

\[
Y_i
=
A_iY_i(1)
+
(1-A_i)Y_i(0)
\]

This allows direct comparison against an oracle policy.

---

# 9. Simulating Organic vs Persuadable Leads

The experiment should explicitly create heterogeneous lead types.

Example segments:

| Segment | Organic Conversion | Uplift |
|---|---:|---:|
| Sure things | high | low |
| Persuadables | medium | high |
| Lost causes | low | low |
| Sleeping dogs | medium | negative |

This creates realistic production behavior.

A standard conversion model naturally prioritizes:
- sure things
- high-intent users

while uplift models prioritize:
- persuadable users

---

# 10. Treatment Assignment Bias

To simulate realistic production systems, treatment assignment should depend on intent features:

\[
e(x)
=
P(A=1\mid X=x)
\]

Example:

\[
e(x)
=
\sigma(
-1
+
1.5 \cdot engagement
+
1.2 \cdot intent
+
0.8 \cdot previous_activity
)
\]

This mimics real sales teams:
- higher-intent leads are more likely to receive contact
- lower-intent leads are often ignored

This creates observational confounding.

---

# 11. Policies Compared

The experiment compares several allocation policies.

---

## Random Allocation

\[
A_i \sim Bernoulli(p)
\]

Serves as baseline.

---

## Conversion Ranking Policy

Rank by:

\[
\hat{s}_i
=
\hat{P}(Y_i=1\mid X_i)
\]

Select top \(B\).

This represents standard production lead scoring.

---

## Oracle Uplift Policy

Rank by true uplift:

\[
\tau_i
\]

This is the optimal allocation policy under equal intervention cost.

---

## Estimated Uplift Policies

Rank by estimated treatment effect:

\[
\hat{\tau}(x)
\]

using:
- T-Learner
- X-Learner
- DR-Learner
- causal forest

These policies approximate the oracle.

---

# 12. Key Evaluation Metrics

Unlike E01, accuracy and precision are no longer sufficient.

E02 evaluates incremental business value.

---

## Incremental Conversions@K

\[
IC@K
=
\sum_{i \in S_K}
\left(
Y_i(1)-Y_i(0)
\right)
\]

Measures additional conversions generated by intervention.

---

## Policy Value

\[
V(\pi)
=
\mathbb{E}[Y(\pi(X))]
\]

Measures expected system outcome under policy \(\pi\).

---

## Regret

\[
R(\pi)
=
V(\pi^*)-V(\pi)
\]

where:
- \(\pi^*\) = oracle allocation policy

Lower regret is better.

---

## AUUC / Qini

These evaluate uplift ranking quality across targeting depths.

They measure whether:
- high predicted uplift
- corresponds to high observed incremental effect

---

# 13. Hypothesis Testing

The central hypothesis is:

\[
H_0:
V(\pi_{uplift})
\le
V(\pi_{conversion})
\]

\[
H_1:
V(\pi_{uplift})
>
V(\pi_{conversion})
\]

Evaluation should use:
- bootstrap confidence intervals
- repeated simulation seeds
- paired policy comparisons

For bootstrap evaluation:

\[
\Delta_b
=
V_b(\pi_{uplift})
-
V_b(\pi_{conversion})
\]

Then estimate:

\[
CI_{95\%}(\Delta)
\]

If the lower bound exceeds zero, uplift allocation demonstrates statistically significant improvement.

---

# 14. Expected Findings

E02 should demonstrate several important phenomena.

---

## Finding 1: High Conversion Does Not Imply High Uplift

Many top-ranked conversion leads are organic converters.

This produces wasted intervention capacity.

---

## Finding 2: Naive Models Overestimate Intervention Value

When treatment assignment is biased:

\[
A_i \not\perp Y_i(0),Y_i(1)
\]

naive outcome comparisons become unreliable.

---

## Finding 3: Uplift Policies Improve Incremental Efficiency

Under fixed sales capacity:

\[
\sum_i A_i \le B
\]

uplift allocation should produce:
- higher incremental conversions
- lower regret
- higher policy value

than conversion ranking.

---

# 15. Why E02 Matters

E01 proved:

> Predictive ranking is better than random ranking.

E02 demonstrates the limitation of that result.

A conversion model can appear highly successful while still allocating resources inefficiently.

This experiment establishes the key causal insight of the project:

\[
P(Y=1\mid X)
\ne
\mathbb{E}[Y(1)-Y(0)\mid X]
\]

or operationally:

> The leads most likely to convert are not necessarily the leads most worth contacting.

That result motivates E03.

---

# 16. Bridge to E03

E03 introduces full causal policy learning and uplift optimization.

Once E02 establishes that:
- exposure bias exists
- conversion ranking wastes capacity
- incremental value differs from raw conversion probability

the next logical step becomes:

> Learn allocation policies that directly optimize incremental business value under operational constraints.

E03 therefore transitions from:
- predictive ranking
- observational analysis
- bias diagnosis

into:
- uplift estimation
- causal allocation
- constrained optimization
- policy learning
- profit-aware intervention systems

Mathematically, E01 optimized:

\[
\max
\sum_i A_i \hat{P}(Y_i=1\mid X_i)
\]

E03 instead optimizes:

\[
\max
\sum_i A_i \hat{\tau}_i
\]

and eventually:

\[
\max
\sum_i A_i
(\hat{\tau}_i v_i - c_i)
\]

under real business constraints.

This is the transition from:
- lead scoring
to:
- causal resource allocation systems.
