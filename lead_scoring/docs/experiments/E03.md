# E02: Uplift Modeling and Policy Benchmarking

## 1. Real-World Problem

A standard lead scoring model ranks leads by:

\[
P(Y=1 \mid X)
\]

This estimates:

> Which leads are likely to convert?

However, sales and marketing teams usually care about a different question:

> Which leads convert because the business intervened?

This distinction matters because some leads would convert organically even without outreach.

If a sales team has limited capacity:

\[
B
\]

then contacting high-probability converters may waste sales effort on customers who would have purchased anyway.

The operational objective is therefore not:

\[
\max_A \sum_i A_i P(Y_i=1 \mid X_i)
\]

Instead, the objective is to maximize the incremental impact caused by intervention.

For example:

- a high-intent lead may convert naturally without a sales call
- a persuadable mid-intent lead may convert only if contacted
- some leads may even respond negatively to intervention

So the system should prioritize:

> incremental value, not raw conversion probability.

This experiment introduces uplift modeling and compares it against ordinary conversion-based ranking.

---

# 2. Mathematical Formulation

## 2.1 Potential Outcomes

For each lead \(i\):

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(X_i\) | feature vector |
| \(A_i \in \{0,1\}\) | intervention decision |
| \(Y_i(1)\) | outcome if intervened |
| \(Y_i(0)\) | outcome without intervention |
| \(Y_i\) | observed outcome |
| \(\tau_i\) | individual treatment effect / uplift |

Observed outcome:

\[
Y_i = A_iY_i(1) + (1-A_i)Y_i(0)
\]

Individual uplift:

\[
\tau_i = Y_i(1)-Y_i(0)
\]

Conditional Average Treatment Effect (CATE):

\[
\tau(x)=\mathbb{E}[Y(1)-Y(0)\mid X=x]
\]

This experiment attempts to estimate:

\[
\hat{\tau}(x)
\]

rather than:

\[
\hat{P}(Y=1\mid X=x)
\]

---

## 2.2 Allocation Objective

Suppose the sales team can contact at most:

\[
B
\]

leads.

The allocation policy chooses:

\[
A_i \in \{0,1\}
\]

subject to:

\[
\sum_i A_i \le B
\]

The optimization objective becomes:

\[
\max_A \sum_i A_i\tau_i
\]

In practice:

\[
\tau_i
\]

is unknown, so the policy uses estimated uplift:

\[
\hat{\tau}_i
\]

The implemented allocation rule is:

\[
S_B^{uplift}
=
\operatorname{TopB}_i(\hat{\tau}_i)
\]

Meaning:

> rank leads by estimated incremental impact and select the top \(B\).

---

## 2.3 Profit-Aware Allocation

If leads have different expected deal values and intervention costs:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(v_i\) | expected lead value |
| \(c_i\) | intervention cost |

then define expected incremental gain:

\[
g_i = \hat{\tau}_i v_i - c_i
\]

The objective becomes:

\[
\max_A \sum_i A_i g_i
\]

subject to:

\[
\sum_i A_i \le B
\]

This is more realistic because:

- not all conversions are equally valuable
- interventions consume business resources
- outreach cost matters operationally

---

# 3. Premise, Assumptions, and Scope

## 3.1 Premise

The central premise is:

> A policy that ranks leads by estimated uplift should produce more incremental business value than a policy that ranks by conversion probability.

This experiment evaluates whether:

\[
V(\pi_{uplift})
>
V(\pi_{conversion})
\]

under the same sales capacity constraint.

---

## 3.2 Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference

For each lead:

\[
Y_i(1)
\]

and:

\[
Y_i(0)
\]

cannot both be observed simultaneously.

Only one outcome is observed:

\[
Y_i = A_iY_i(1)+(1-A_i)Y_i(0)
\]

Therefore:

\[
\tau_i
\]

is fundamentally unobserved.

This experiment estimates treatment effects statistically.

---

## 3.3 Assumptions

### A1. Consistency

Observed outcomes correspond to realized treatment.

If:

\[
A_i=1
\]

then:

\[
Y_i=Y_i(1)
\]

If:

\[
A_i=0
\]

then:

\[
Y_i=Y_i(0)
\]

---

### A2. Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA)

One lead’s treatment does not affect another lead’s outcome.

Formally:

\[
Y_i(a)
\]

depends only on lead \(i\)'s own treatment assignment.

---

### A3. Ignorability / Unconfoundedness

Treatment assignment is independent of potential outcomes conditional on observed features:

\[
(Y_i(1),Y_i(0)) \perp A_i \mid X_i
\]

This assumption is strongest under randomized experiments.

---

### A4. Overlap / Positivity

Every lead must have nonzero probability of receiving either treatment:

\[
0 < P(A=1\mid X=x) < 1
\]

Without overlap, uplift cannot be estimated reliably.

---

### A5. Temporal Ordering

Features must be measured before treatment and outcome windows.

This avoids leakage.

---

# 4. Why This Experiment Matters

## 4.1 Conversion Probability Is Not Incremental Impact

A standard lead scorer estimates:

\[
P(Y=1\mid X)
\]

But a lead may convert even without intervention.

A high conversion score does not imply:

\[
Y(1)-Y(0)
\]

is large.

Example:

| Lead Type | Conversion Probability | Uplift |
|---|---:|---:|
| Organic buyer | high | low |
| Persuadable lead | medium | high |
| Bad fit | low | negative |

A conversion ranker over-selects organic buyers.

An uplift ranker attempts to identify persuadable leads.

---

## 4.2 The Business Objective Is Incrementality

Sales teams care about:

- incremental conversions
- incremental revenue
- return on sales effort
- efficient capacity allocation

Therefore the correct optimization target is:

\[
\sum_i A_i\tau_i
\]

not merely:

\[
\sum_i A_iP(Y_i=1\mid X_i)
\]

---

## 4.3 Uplift Modeling Creates Better Resource Allocation

Under limited capacity:

\[
\sum_i A_i \le B
\]

only a small subset of leads can be contacted.

A better ranking policy can:

- improve incremental conversions
- reduce wasted outreach
- improve revenue per intervention
- improve sales efficiency

---

## 4.4 This Experiment Introduces Causal Decision Making

This experiment moves the project from:

> predictive modeling

into:

> causal allocation and policy optimization.

That is a major conceptual shift.

---

# 5. Experimental Design

## 5.1 Datasets

This experiment should use either:

1. Hillstrom / MineThatData randomized marketing dataset
2. semi-synthetic lead allocation dataset
3. both datasets together

Recommended:

- Hillstrom for realism
- semi-synthetic data for oracle benchmarking

---

## 5.2 Semi-Synthetic Data Generating Process

Semi-synthetic data is useful because true uplift is known.

### Step 1: Generate baseline conversion probability

Define:

\[
p_0(x)=P(Y(0)=1\mid X=x)
\]

Example:

\[
p_0(x)=
\sigma(
-4
+0.8\cdot engagement
+1.2\cdot otp
+0.5\cdot form
)
\]

---

### Step 2: Generate true uplift

Define:

\[
\tau(x)
\]

Example:

\[
\tau(x)=
0.02
+0.10\cdot mid\_intent
-0.08\cdot high\_intent
\]

This creates heterogeneous treatment effects.

---

### Step 3: Generate treated probability

\[
p_1(x)=clip(p_0(x)+\tau(x),0,1)
\]

---

### Step 4: Assign treatment

Randomized assignment:

\[
A_i \sim Bernoulli(0.5)
\]

Observational assignment:

\[
e(x)=P(A=1\mid X=x)
\]

Example:

\[
e(x)=\sigma(-1+1.5\cdot engagement)
\]

---

### Step 5: Generate observed outcomes

\[
Y_i \sim Bernoulli(A_ip_1(x)+(1-A_i)p_0(x))
\]

---

# 6. Models To Benchmark

## 6.1 Random Allocation

Baseline random policy:

\[
S_B^{random}
\sim Uniform
\]

This provides the minimum benchmark.

---

## 6.2 Conversion Ranking Baseline

Train:

\[
\hat{s}(x)=\hat{P}(Y=1\mid X=x)
\]

Allocate:

\[
S_B^{score}
=
\operatorname{TopB}(\hat{s})
\]

This represents ordinary lead scoring.

---

## 6.3 T-Learner

Train two separate outcome models:

\[
\hat{\mu}_1(x)=\mathbb{E}[Y\mid X=x,A=1]
\]

\[
\hat{\mu}_0(x)=\mathbb{E}[Y\mid X=x,A=0]
\]

Estimate uplift:

\[
\hat{\tau}(x)=\hat{\mu}_1(x)-\hat{\mu}_0(x)
\]

Allocate top uplift leads.

---

## 6.4 X-Learner or DR-Learner

Advanced causal meta-learners may also be benchmarked.

These are especially useful when:

- treatment groups are imbalanced
- treatment assignment is biased
- observational data is used

---

## 6.5 Oracle Policy

For semi-synthetic data, true uplift is known:

\[
\tau_i
\]

The oracle policy selects:

\[
S_B^{oracle}
=
\operatorname{TopB}(\tau_i)
\]

This provides the theoretical upper bound.

---

# 7. Allocation Policies

The experiment should compare:

| Policy | Description |
|---|---|
| Random | random lead selection |
| Conversion Top-K | rank by conversion probability |
| Uplift Top-K | rank by estimated uplift |
| Profit-aware uplift | rank by expected incremental profit |
| Oracle | rank by true uplift |

Run all policies under identical capacity constraints.

Example:

\[
B \in \{50,100,200,500\}
\]

---

# 8. Evaluation Metrics

## 8.1 Incremental Conversions@K

Measures incremental conversions generated by the selected policy.

---

## 8.2 Policy Value

Define policy value:

\[
V(\pi)
=
\mathbb{E}[Y(\pi(X))]
\]

For semi-synthetic data:

\[
V(\pi)
=
\sum_i A_i\tau_i
\]

Higher policy value is better.

---

## 8.3 Regret

Define oracle regret:

\[
R(\pi)=V(\pi^*)-V(\pi)
\]

where:

\[
\pi^*
\]

is the oracle allocation policy.

Lower regret is better.

---

## 8.4 AUUC

Area Under the Uplift Curve.

Measures ranking quality of uplift estimates.

Higher AUUC indicates better uplift ranking.

---

## 8.5 Qini Curve

Measures cumulative incremental gain as more leads are targeted.

Useful for visualizing uplift ranking quality.

---

# 9. Hypothesis Testing

## 9.1 Main Hypothesis

Null hypothesis:

\[
H_0:
V(\pi_{uplift})
\le
V(\pi_{conversion})
\]

Alternative hypothesis:

\[
H_1:
V(\pi_{uplift})
>
V(\pi_{conversion})
\]

Interpretation:

> uplift allocation creates more incremental business value than conversion ranking.

---

## 9.2 Bootstrap Confidence Intervals

For each bootstrap sample:

1. resample leads with replacement
2. recompute policy values
3. compute:

\[
\Delta_b
=
V_b(\pi_{uplift})
-
V_b(\pi_{conversion})
\]

Then estimate:

\[
CI_{95\%}(\Delta)
\]

If the lower bound exceeds zero, uplift allocation significantly outperforms conversion ranking.

# 11. What This Experiment Can and Cannot Prove

## It Can Prove

- uplift modeling improves incremental allocation quality
- conversion ranking is not equivalent to uplift ranking
- incremental value can differ from conversion probability
- causal allocation policies outperform random selection
- profit-aware allocation improves business efficiency

---

## It Cannot Fully Prove

- causal validity under strong hidden confounding
- long-term online performance
- stability under distribution shift
- optimality under sequential interventions

Those extensions belong to later experiments.

# 12. Previous Work and Related Literature

## 12.1 Uplift Modeling

Uplift modeling estimates differential treatment effects rather than raw outcome probability.

Core insight:

> the best target is not necessarily the most likely converter.

Useful references:

- Devriendt et al., “Learning to rank for uplift modeling”
- Radcliffe and Surry, uplift marketing literature
- scikit-uplift documentation

---

## 12.2 Meta-Learners for Treatment Effects

Modern causal ML decomposes treatment-effect estimation into supervised learning subproblems.

Important learners:

| Method | Main Idea |
|---|---|
| S-Learner | single model with treatment feature |
| T-Learner | separate treatment/control models |
| X-Learner | treatment-effect imputation |
| R-Learner | orthogonalized residual learning |
| DR-Learner | doubly robust estimation |

Key reference:

- Künzel et al., “Metalearners for estimating heterogeneous treatment effects”

Insight:

> treatment-effect estimation is different from ordinary prediction.

---

## 12.3 Causal Forests

Causal forests estimate heterogeneous treatment effects directly.

Key reference:

- Athey, Tibshirani, Wager, generalized random forests

Insight:

> treatment effects vary across subpopulations.

---

## 12.4 Policy Learning

Modern policy learning directly optimizes intervention allocation.

Key reference:

- Athey and Wager, policy learning with observational data

Insight:

> decision quality matters more than prediction quality.

---

## 12.5 Offline Policy Evaluation

Offline policy evaluation estimates the value of a policy using historical data.

Important estimators:

- IPS
- doubly robust estimators
- augmented inverse propensity weighting

Insight:

> policy evaluation requires correcting for biased treatment assignment.

---

# 13. Final Experiment Statement

This experiment evaluates whether uplift modeling creates better sales allocation policies than ordinary conversion ranking.

The system estimates:

\[
\hat{\tau}(x)=\mathbb{E}[Y(1)-Y(0)\mid X=x]
\]

rather than:

\[
P(Y=1\mid X=x)
\]

The allocation policy selects:

\[
S_B^{uplift}
=
\operatorname{TopB}(\hat{\tau})
\]

under the capacity constraint:

\[
\sum_i A_i \le B
\]

The experiment is successful if uplift-based allocation produces:

- higher incremental conversions
- higher policy value
- lower regret
- better AUUC/Qini

than conversion-based ranking under the same operational budget.

This experiment establishes the transition from predictive lead scoring to causal allocation and policy optimization.
